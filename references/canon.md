# Mission-Driven Morphogenesis Theory Canon

Source note: this canon packages a theory originally supplied in Japanese. The
title is normalized to ASCII here to keep Windows-side validation stable.

## One-Line Thesis

The form of an artifact is a compressed sign of mission, origin, environment,
operation, and history, reconciled with readability and production reality.

## Formal Model

Working ASCII notation: `F = Phi(C, R, K)`

Source note: the original notation used a Greek capital phi operator. This
reference normalizes it to `Phi` for tool compatibility.

- `F`: final form
- `C`: context-causal system
- `R`: readability system
- `K`: production-constraint system
- `Phi`: the design process that mediates conflicting demands into one form

This is not arithmetic. `Phi` is the act of reconciling competing demands until
they converge into one convincing silhouette, structure, and surface language.

## Systems

### Context-Causal System

`C = {M, O, E, U, H}`

- `M`: mission
- `O`: origin
- `E`: environment
- `U`: operation
- `H`: history

This system answers: why must the artifact take this form at all?

### Readability System

`R = {S, P, Q, D}`

- `S`: silhouette
- `P`: proportions
- `Q`: meaning-signs
- `D`: detail hierarchy

This system answers: can a viewer read the intended meaning quickly?

### Production-Constraint System

`K = {A, G, B, X, Y}`

- `A`: material
- `G`: process
- `B`: budget and time
- `X`: medium
- `Y`: body and safety conditions

This system answers: can the artifact be made, staged, animated, or shipped in
the stated reality?

## Core Definitions

### Artifact

An artifact is any human-made object or system: tool, vehicle, weapon,
architecture, interface, costume, stage object, industrial device, or similar.

### Form

Form is not only outer shape. It includes outline, proportions, section,
layout, construction, surface condition, and detail hierarchy.

### Mission

Mission is the total role an artifact must fulfill in a specific situation.
Transport, suppression, rescue, intimidation, ceremony, governance, display,
status signaling, and infiltration are all mission types.

### Function

Function is the local action of a part or mechanism that helps the mission
succeed.

### Central Distinction

- Macro form follows mission, environment, and use situation.
- Micro structure follows function, ergonomics, and operation.

Overall silhouette, massing, stance, and major layout come from mission.
Hatches, handles, vents, access panels, sights, grips, and maintenance points
come from function and operation.

## Context-Causal Factors

### Mission

Mission is the highest-order cause. It decides what must read as fast, durable,
threatening, stable, covert, prestigious, or load-bearing.

### Origin

Origin is the production culture behind the artifact: who made it, with what
technology, standards, resources, and values. Mass production, scarcity,
bureaucratic standardization, field improvisation, and craft culture all leave
visible traces in construction.

### Environment

Environment includes physical and social conditions: cold, desert, vacuum,
water, high gravity, urban density, law, infrastructure, and culture.

### Operation

Operation covers deployment, maintenance, transport, storage, supply, crew
count, replacement cycle, and field handling. It explains access, weight
distribution, packing, disassembly, and service logic.

### History

History is visible time: wear, repair, retrofits, replacement parts, patina, or
strict maintenance that keeps an object pristine. Untouched condition is also a
form of history if a system enforces it.

## Readability Factors

### Silhouette

Silhouette is the first readable signal. At a glance it should suggest speed,
mass, danger, stability, civilian use, military use, ritual purpose, or other
high-level character.

### Proportions

Proportions communicate weight, agility, grace, intimidation, fragility, and
competence.

### Meaning-Signs

Meaning-signs are the cues that let viewers infer danger, trust, authority,
cheapness, provisionality, prestige, age, or modernity. Readability is not
decoration. It is compressed communication.

### Detail Hierarchy

Different distances should reveal different information:

- Far distance: outline and overall character
- Mid distance: major layout and use logic
- Near distance: joins, service logic, material, and history

Good detail is not maximum density. It is correctly sorted information by
viewing distance.

## Production-Constraint Factors

### Material

Material sets believable thickness, curvature, joining method, durability, and
surface treatment.

### Process

Process limits complexity, precision, part segmentation, and feasible geometry.

### Budget and Time

Budget and time limit part count, detail density, finish quality, mechanical
complexity, and number of iterations.

### Medium

Film, games, animation, industrial production, stage use, and illustration all
need different information densities, viewing distances, and motion behavior.

### Body and Safety

If people must touch, ride, wear, aim, lift, or avoid it, reach, grip,
visibility, boarding, exit, and hazard conditions must be credible.

## Generation Order

Use this order when creating form:

1. Define the mission in one sentence.
2. Define origin.
3. Define environment and operation.
4. Write down production constraints.
5. Draft macro silhouette and massing.
6. Decide major structure and layout.
7. Add local structures from function and operation.
8. Add history.
9. Adjust readability.

This order means: decide why it exists before deciding how it should read.

## Mediation Order

When demands conflict, resolve them in this order:

1. Avoid implementation failure, danger, or medium breakage.
2. Preserve mission-environment coherence.
3. Preserve operation-function coherence.
4. Preserve origin consistency.
5. Preserve historical and surface plausibility.
6. Maximize readability last.

Readability matters, but not at the cost of breaking internal causality.

## Core Propositions

1. Macro form arises primarily from mission and environment, not from the sum
   of local functions.
2. Local function governs micro structure but does not determine total form by
   itself.
3. Origin appears in construction, and construction makes production culture
   visible.
4. Operation determines layout, and layout tells the story of use.
5. History is visible time, and pristine condition is also a managed history.
6. Readability is a design condition for transmitting causality, not ornament.
7. Production constraints are first-order causes of form, not mere compromise.
8. Beauty appears as a byproduct when causality, meaning, and feasibility align.

## Evaluation Tests

### Three-Second Readability

Can a viewer roughly infer purpose or temperament in three seconds?

### Ten-Second Origin Guess

Can a viewer infer who made it and what kind of industrial culture produced it?

### Thirty-Second Operation Guess

Can a viewer infer boarding, maintenance, fragile zones, or handling logic?

### Causal Trace

Can each major form decision be explained by mission, origin, environment,
operation, history, readability, or production constraint?

### Subtraction Test

If removing a detail makes the design stronger, that detail was noise.

### Replacement Test

If swapping mission or origin leaves the form largely unchanged, the design is
probably under-rooted in context.

## Scope

This theory is especially strong for:

- vehicles
- weapons
- tools
- architecture
- industrial products
- stage devices
- film and game artifact design
- worldbuilding-driven concept design

## Limits

- It does not produce a single correct answer.
- It does not replace engineering validation.
- Readability is partly culture-dependent.
- Abstract or intentionally deceptive design may invert readability on purpose.
